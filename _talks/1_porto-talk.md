The Neurophysiology of Wine Tasting was an invited seminar delivered for Sogrape Wines in Porto, introducing contemporary neuroscience perspectives on flavour perception to wine professionals. Rather than viewing wine tasting as a passive detection of aromas and tastes, the lecture presented flavour as an active cognitive construction emerging from the integration of olfactory, gustatory, trigeminal, somatosensory, and contextual information within the brain. This constructivist framework reflects recent advances in neuroenology and cognitive neuroscience, positioning wine perception as a dynamic interaction between sensory input, memory, attention, expectation, and expertise.

The seminar explored the complete neurophysiology of wine perception—from the molecular basis of aroma detection and the distinction between orthonasal and retronasal olfaction, to the neural mechanisms underlying taste, mouthfeel, astringency, and multisensory integration. Particular emphasis was placed on the concept of the "flavour image": the distributed neural representation through which the brain integrates sensory signals into the unified percept we experience as wine. Recent neuroimaging studies of sommeliers were discussed to illustrate how expertise alters the neural processing of flavour, supporting the idea that expert tasting reflects learned perceptual organisation rather than heightened sensory acuity alone.

Beyond the scientific foundations, the lecture considered how sensory neuroscience can inform professional wine practice. Topics included sensory adaptation and fatigue, retronasal aroma perception, glassware and aroma release, individual variability in flavour perception, the neurobiology of mouthfeel, and evidence-based approaches to sensory training. By connecting contemporary neuroscience with oenology, the seminar demonstrated how understanding the mechanisms of flavour perception can improve sensory evaluation, communication, and expertise development within the wine industry.
