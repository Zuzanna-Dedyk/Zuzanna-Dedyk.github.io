I led this workshop for the Donders Focus Group on Olfaction in Cognition, Brain, and Behaviour, inviting neuroscientists and olfaction researchers to evaluate specialty coffee using the same protocols professional judges use in competition. The premise was simple: give people trained to study perception a chance to navigate sensory complexity in real time, with real stimuli, under real constraints.

Analytical tools can tell us a great deal about coffee — gas chromatography identifies volatile compounds, refractometers measure dissolved solids, pH meters track acidity — but none of them can predict whether a coffee will taste fruity or floral, or whether anyone will actually want to drink it. Professional coffee judges work with a different kind of complexity: managing high-dimensional sensory information, across dozens of samples, that exceeds the resolution of current analytical methods.

We cupped coffee using the Specialty Coffee Association's Coffee Value Assessment protocol, which deliberately separates objective sensory description from affective preference. What emerged was a striking pattern: some participants could clearly detect differences between coffees but struggled to name what they were tasting, while experienced tasters reached for precise but insider language — "jammy stone fruit," "structured acidity" — meaningful to other judges, opaque to everyone else. This wasn't necessarily a perception gap. It looked more like a vocabulary gap.

That distinction raises a question I keep returning to: how much of sensory expertise is really language acquisition — learning to map complex experience onto a shared vocabulary — rather than perceptual sensitivity itself? Flavour lexicons rely on reference standards, but that assumes everyone perceives the reference identically. My "strawberry" might sit closer to your "wild berries" than to your "strawberry." We may be using shared terms while experiencing subtly different perceptual realities — which suggests that calibration may align our language more than it aligns our perception.

No firm conclusions yet — just better questions, and ones I intend to keep pursuing.
