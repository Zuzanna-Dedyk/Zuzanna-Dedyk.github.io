#**Exploring how the brain constructs perceptual reality through flavour, imagery, and altered states**

How does the brain decide what is real? Perception is not a passive recording of the external world — it emerges from a dynamic interaction between sensory input, prior knowledge, expectations, and internal representations. The Latent States Project investigates how these hidden cognitive processes shape conscious experience, using flavour perception as a unique window into the construction of reality.

As part of the Latent States programme at the Champalimaud Foundation, this research explores human perception in naturalistic environments, bringing neuroscience beyond traditional laboratory settings. Through field-based experiments conducted at Waking Life Festival (Portugal), we investigate how altered states of consciousness influence multisensory perception, mental imagery, and the interaction between sensory evidence and internal predictions.

My work within this project focuses on chemosensory perception — the perception of aroma and flavour — a domain where sensory signals are inherently ambiguous and strongly shaped by memory, expectations, and imagination. Using guided coffee-tasting paradigms, we examine how altered states influence cross-modal associations, sensory discrimination, and the tendency for imagined sensory features to become incorporated into perceptual experience.

This research provides new insights into the mechanisms underlying perception, revealing how the brain balances bottom-up sensory information with top-down cognitive processes to construct our subjective experience of the world.

**Methods:** naturalistic sensory experiments, coffee tasting, psychophysics, flavour perception, mental imagery, cross-modal perception, computational modelling.

**Collaborators:** Systems Neuroscience Lab, Champalimaud Foundation; Latent States programme.
